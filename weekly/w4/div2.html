Aesthetic Usability Effect

Users often perceive aesthetically
pleasing design as design that's
more usable.

Aesthetically pleasing design can make users more
tolerant of minor usability issues.

Aesthetically pleasing design can mask usability problems
and prevent issues from being discovered during usability
testing.

-ORIGINS

The aesthetic usability effect was first studied in the field of
human-computer interaction in 1995. Reasearchers Masaaki
Kurosu and Kaori Kashimura from the Hitachi Design Center
tested 26 variations of an ATM UI, asking the 252 study
participants to rate each design on ease of use, as well as
aestetic appeal. They found a stronger correlation between the
participants' rathings of aesthetic appeal and perceived ease of use
than the correlation between their ratings of aesthetic appeal and
actual ease of use. Kurosu and Kashimura conclueded that users
are strongly influenced by the aesthetics of any given interface,
even when they try to evaluate the underlying functionality of the
system.
